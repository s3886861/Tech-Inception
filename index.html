IT Work


What type of work is done by the IT professional?

Mr Adrian Beck is the Director of Information Systems at St Mary’s Anglican Girl’s School in Western Australia. His main role within IT is to oversee all industry related projects on behalf of the principal and the school. Some of these projects have him supervise the installation and upgrade of all audio/visual components in 31 different classrooms or testing new network security features, that before his time, were not possible. Mr Beck believes that a “stronger security posture” needs to be adopted within the school. Current security measures are “more reactive as opposed to proactive”, so the IT team has been busy conducting password sprays and a simulated dictionary attack to test for further vulnerabilities. Mr Beck’s main focus is predominantly within the IT department, managing his team and ensuring that work is being carried out efficiently. Although, he has another key appointment that is essential to the running of the school – being a member of the Executive Board. This role has other unique responsibilities that include budget management, meetings with the Principal, the Deans, and the Director of Business Operations. At the same time, it also allows Mr Beck to have more insight as to how the school is operating and have a voice in the adoption and implementation of new Technologies in the school.


What kinds of people does the IT professional interact with?

As Mr Beck has multiple roles at St Mary’s Anglican Girls School, he interacts and communicates with different types of professionals at different intervals throughout the day. Currently Mr Beck leads a team of seven IT professionals including four Technicians, one Helpdesk Operator and one Network Manager. Mr Beck also has the opportunity to interact with external contractors who carry out work for the school. Stepping away from the IT department, Mr Beck communicates with a range of other constituents ranging from students, parents and staff members, to the Executive board which hosts individuals such as the Principal, Vice-Principal, Deans and Business Managers. Mr Beck also regularly meets with sales representatives from IT Businesses, Products and Software vendors to discuss the needs of the school. Lastly, he stays in constant communication with his peers at other schools around the country with the help of a mailing list that allows them to request assistance for issues, share ideas and implementation details for various technologies, compare what tools they use and why, and stay abreast of any major developments that he needs to be aware of.


Where does the IT professional spend most of their time?

IT professionals spend a great deal of time during the day monitoring systems and responding to technical issues. Depending on their position, their work would differ in its scope and complexity. In the case of Mr Beck’s workplace at St Mary’s, an IT Technician would spend much of their day responding to urgent calls for assistance from staff members and students. This involves moving around the school campus to assist with projector connection problems, wireless issues, printing difficulties and software difficulties. The Helpdesk Officer deals mainly with support calls over the phone and assists people who come to visit the Helpdesk Office in person. The Network Manager monitors the school network hardware as well as the flow of data, wireless systems, servers and looks for ways to improve them. Mr Beck as the Director of IT partakes in various meetings and spends much of his time working on larger projects which include school reports as well as the school IT Budget. They all work together as a team and support each other when needed. Another common aspect of most IT positions is to research and expand their knowledge where possible. There is always a solution to every problem and there is always more to learn.


What aspect of their position is most challenging?

Mr Beck identified that his biggest challenges within the workplace lie within the school’s budget allocation and cybersecurity threats. As a member of the Executive board, Mr Beck’s role is to help allocate funds to different departments around the school. IT being a resource intensive department, the school’s allocated budget needs to be regularly monitored and shared according to the business direction provided by the school Principal. Budgets always start out very large as all the requests pile in and then go through many reviews and revisions where decisions have to be made as to what to keep, what to delay and what to cut. It is very time consuming and highly stressful.
However, when compared to budget concerns, cybersecurity is Mr Beck’s biggest worry and the most challenging problem in the workplace. Mr Beck regularly thinks “What's happening with malware? What are the current threats that are actually taking place while I'm asleep?”. Then of course, questions start to arise, “Do we have enough protection? Are our staff suitably skilled?”. According to Mr Beck, the weakest point in security is the staff. Training them to be security conscious is a never-ending battle and Mr Beck realizes that it is one of the most challenging aspects of his work. Security is the opposite of convenience and while some staff might find it easier to login to their workstations with a simple password such as Allan246, it will not stop a hacker from brute forcing that password quickly and gaining access to the network to infect it with ransomware. The dangers this threat pose to the business as a whole is quite devastating both financially and in terms of reputation. As the threat landscape increases and matures, its more and more important to work proactively to combat this issue. It is quite challenging indeed and as Mr Beck indicated to us in his interview, his goal is to employ an IT Professional with a background in Cybersecurity to work with him and his team to proactively monitor and respond to security threats to the school.

Autonomous Vehicles

Autonomous Vehicles (AVs) can be described as any vehicle that has the capability for motion and action with little to no human interference, whether that interference be remote or from inside the vehicle itself (Frazzoli, Dahleh, & Feron, 2002). Though the idea of them may seem futuristic, evidence of AVs exists as far back as 1986 at Carnegie-Mellon University in the form of the Navlab prototypes, though the functionality at the time was extremely basic due to the lack of compact computing power (Jochem, Pomerleau, Kumar & Armstrong, 1995). The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE International, 2018) has outlined the 6 levels of vehicle automation from 0 to 5, with 0 having no automation and 5 being fully autonomous. Since the Navlab experiments at Carnegie-Mellon University, autonomous driving technology has come a long way and is now available to the consumer market from various automotive companies. Tesla’s Autopilot and General Motors’ (GM) Super Cruise are two such offerings, though currently they only offer level 2 automation. As can be seen in Figure 1, this is classed as partial automation and still requires constant monitoring by the driver.

SAE Levels of Driving Automation Figure 1 (Synopsys, 2020)

Though there are AVs capable of level 4 automation, outdated legislation and infrastructure limits where they are safely able to function in a process known as geofencing (Synopsys, 2020). In 2018, an AV company by the name of Waymo announced a driverless taxi service in Phoenix, Arizona that would operate in four suburbs: Chandler, Tempe, Gilbert and Mesa (Sage, 2018). Initially, this service had a human behind the wheel to ensure a safe trip for passengers but, by the end of 2020 it was able to transition to a near driverless experience with each vehicle having a remote supervisor to assist when more dynamic situations would arise. For example, a Waymo spokesperson explained to Lee (2020) ‘if a Waymo vehicle detects that a road ahead may be closed due to construction, it can pull over and request a second set of eyes’. Waymo’s choice to start rolling out their service in these Phoenix suburbs is not an irrelevant detail as weather conditions play a big part in how well AVs operate.

In the next 3 years and further moving forward, large amounts of autonomous vehicles are expected to be rapidly deployed around the world with the rise of sensor technologies and Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs). ABI Research (2018) predicts that approximately 8 million vehicles featuring level 3, 4 and 5 autonomous functions will be shipped in 2025 and available to the mass market. While the vehicles featuring level 3 and 4 AV functions may still need drivers due to certain driving conditions requiring human intervention, vehicles with level 5 autonomy will be able to operate completely autonomously with no driver intervention at all.

Other sources, such as Underwood (2014), predict that AVs in 2025 may be able to operate on all forms of road – whether it be highway, urban or rural surfaces. They also predict full level 5 autonomy on all surfaces by at least 2035, effectively eliminating humans from behind the wheel. Wardrop (2009) proposes that by 2022, 30% of all trucks operating in the United Kingdom have the potential to be fully automated while IEEE (2012) estimates that up to 75% of all vehicles on the road could be fully autonomous by 2040. In the transport and haulage industry, trucks and buses may be the first to see autonomous replacements as the routes taken are far less dynamic than those taken by private commuters.

More and more countries are beginning to legalise the use of AVs on their roads such as the UK, Sweden, and the US. In 2017, Sweden launched the Drive Me project which saw 100 Volvo AVs go to families in Gothenburg. These vehicles were for use on public roads and did not require continuous supervision, allowing the driver to perform other tasks during their trips (Victor et al., 2017). With the research gathered from these countries, there may be more AVs on roads for our use sooner rather than later.

The quick rise and development of AVs for use on roads is possible due to advancements in technologies such as LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) sensors. LiDAR has the potential to provide reliable obstacle detection, coupled with the deployment of various Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) packages by OEMs.

The technological developments of vehicle automation potentially can transform the workforce and impact jobs. The potential impact of automation on employment varies by occupation and sector, with more physical jobs in predictable environments being the most susceptible. These may include operating machinery and preparing fast food. Two more industries that may be affected by automation are collecting and processing data, with jobs such as mortgage origination, paralegal work, accounting, and back-office transaction processing seeing a significant decrease in available work (McKinsey & Co, 2017) due to the evolution of not just vehicle automation, but automation as a whole.

AVs could cause a rise in energy consumption levels when they become the prevalent mode of transport. This predicted rise may be caused by an increased demand for travel due to the on-demand mobility they offer, which in turn increases the amount of energy used by AVs. Another driving factor for this rise may be because of the accessibility these vehicles provide, allowing people who do not drive as much or not have been driving for a period of time to make use of the services offered by AVs.

At the same time, AVs may be able to help energy consumption due to efficient driving methods, through a concept known as platooning. Platooning is when multiple vehicles all operate in a tight group together which helps to reduce forces imposed upon the vehicles (especially those in the center) while driving. This method of driving is not possible for humans due to the reaction times required to brake without causing a crash. AVs would not have this issue due to their ability to communicate and execute commands based on their surroundings (Wadud, MacKenzie & Leiby, 2016).

The establishment of AVs as the conventional method of both private and public transportation is still several years from fruition. Though level 5 autonomy is on the horizon, current AVs are only able to operate in a limited capacity – whether this limit be geofenced areas or the requirement of constant human oversight. As the adoption rate of AVs increases, positives and negatives may become more apparent in day-to-day life. Some such benefits may include a decrease in traffic congestion, increased mobility for people with disabilities or the elderly, and an overall safer experience for both pedestrians and drivers. With over 90% of crashes caused by human error (NHTSA, 2015) the adoption of AVs has the potential for a large positive impact on road safety due to the communicative ability they possess with each other. However, accidents are not always avoidable.

AVs may encounter ethical issues relating to the safety of the driver versus the safety of pedestrians in impact scenarios. In these types of scenarios, a computer may make unpredictable decisions and end up causing more harm than a human driver. While there are benefits to consider, there are also negative aspects of AVs, such as the threat of cybersecurity related attacks. A person with malicious intent may be able to compromise an AV to disable safety features and cause an impact, steal private information such as where an individual may live or work, or reroute the destination of the AV. Due to the relatively small demand for AVs, another factor that is against them is the cost. Without a large network of AVs in operation and because they rely on communication within a network, congestion mitigation could be completely nullified due to the lack of information available. Weather and terrain may also have an adverse effect on AVs, with the functionality being reduced or even fully negated due to rain, snow or uneven terrain (Anderson et al., 2014). Until these issues are investigated further, autonomous driving may still be years away from being available to the public at large.
