IT Work


What type of work is done by the IT professional?

Mr Adrian Beck is the Director of Information Systems at St Mary’s Anglican Girl’s School in Western Australia. His main role within IT is to oversee all industry related projects on behalf of the principal and the school. Some of these projects have him supervise the installation and upgrade of all audio/visual components in 31 different classrooms or testing new network security features, that before his time, were not possible. Mr Beck believes that a “stronger security posture” needs to be adopted within the school. Current security measures are “more reactive as opposed to proactive”, so the IT team has been busy conducting password sprays and a simulated dictionary attack to test for further vulnerabilities. Mr Beck’s main focus is predominantly within the IT department, managing his team and ensuring that work is being carried out efficiently. Although, he has another key appointment that is essential to the running of the school – being a member of the Executive Board. This role has other unique responsibilities that include budget management, meetings with the Principal, the Deans, and the Director of Business Operations. At the same time, it also allows Mr Beck to have more insight as to how the school is operating and have a voice in the adoption and implementation of new Technologies in the school.


What kinds of people does the IT professional interact with?

As Mr Beck has multiple roles at St Mary’s Anglican Girls School, he interacts and communicates with different types of professionals at different intervals throughout the day. Currently Mr Beck leads a team of seven IT professionals including four Technicians, one Helpdesk Operator and one Network Manager. Mr Beck also has the opportunity to interact with external contractors who carry out work for the school. Stepping away from the IT department, Mr Beck communicates with a range of other constituents ranging from students, parents and staff members, to the Executive board which hosts individuals such as the Principal, Vice-Principal, Deans and Business Managers. Mr Beck also regularly meets with sales representatives from IT Businesses, Products and Software vendors to discuss the needs of the school. Lastly, he stays in constant communication with his peers at other schools around the country with the help of a mailing list that allows them to request assistance for issues, share ideas and implementation details for various technologies, compare what tools they use and why, and stay abreast of any major developments that he needs to be aware of.


Where does the IT professional spend most of their time?

IT professionals spend a great deal of time during the day monitoring systems and responding to technical issues. Depending on their position, their work would differ in its scope and complexity. In the case of Mr Beck’s workplace at St Mary’s, an IT Technician would spend much of their day responding to urgent calls for assistance from staff members and students. This involves moving around the school campus to assist with projector connection problems, wireless issues, printing difficulties and software difficulties. The Helpdesk Officer deals mainly with support calls over the phone and assists people who come to visit the Helpdesk Office in person. The Network Manager monitors the school network hardware as well as the flow of data, wireless systems, servers and looks for ways to improve them. Mr Beck as the Director of IT partakes in various meetings and spends much of his time working on larger projects which include school reports as well as the school IT Budget. They all work together as a team and support each other when needed. Another common aspect of most IT positions is to research and expand their knowledge where possible. There is always a solution to every problem and there is always more to learn.


What aspect of their position is most challenging?

Mr Beck identified that his biggest challenges within the workplace lie within the school’s budget allocation and cybersecurity threats. As a member of the Executive board, Mr Beck’s role is to help allocate funds to different departments around the school. IT being a resource intensive department, the school’s allocated budget needs to be regularly monitored and shared according to the business direction provided by the school Principal. Budgets always start out very large as all the requests pile in and then go through many reviews and revisions where decisions have to be made as to what to keep, what to delay and what to cut. It is very time consuming and highly stressful.
However, when compared to budget concerns, cybersecurity is Mr Beck’s biggest worry and the most challenging problem in the workplace. Mr Beck regularly thinks “What's happening with malware? What are the current threats that are actually taking place while I'm asleep?”. Then of course, questions start to arise, “Do we have enough protection? Are our staff suitably skilled?”. According to Mr Beck, the weakest point in security is the staff. Training them to be security conscious is a never-ending battle and Mr Beck realizes that it is one of the most challenging aspects of his work. Security is the opposite of convenience and while some staff might find it easier to login to their workstations with a simple password such as Allan246, it will not stop a hacker from brute forcing that password quickly and gaining access to the network to infect it with ransomware. The dangers this threat pose to the business as a whole is quite devastating both financially and in terms of reputation. As the threat landscape increases and matures, its more and more important to work proactively to combat this issue. It is quite challenging indeed and as Mr Beck indicated to us in his interview, his goal is to employ an IT Professional with a background in Cybersecurity to work with him and his team to proactively monitor and respond to security threats to the school.

IT Technologies


Autonomous Vehicles (AVs) can be described as any vehicle that has the capability for motion and action with little to no human interference, whether that interference be remote or from inside the vehicle itself (Frazzoli, Dahleh, & Feron, 2002). Though the idea of them may seem futuristic, evidence of AVs exists as far back as 1986 at Carnegie-Mellon University in the form of the Navlab prototypes, though the functionality at the time was extremely basic due to the lack of compact computing power (Jochem, Pomerleau, Kumar & Armstrong, 1995). The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE International, 2018) has outlined the 6 levels of vehicle automation from 0 to 5, with 0 having no automation and 5 being fully autonomous. Since the Navlab experiments at Carnegie-Mellon University, autonomous driving technology has come a long way and is now available to the consumer market from various automotive companies. Tesla’s Autopilot and General Motors’ (GM) Super Cruise are two such offerings, though currently they only offer level 2 automation. As can be seen in Figure 1, this is classed as partial automation and still requires constant monitoring by the driver.

Figure 1 (Synopsys, 2020)

Though there are AVs capable of level 4 automation, outdated legislation and infrastructure limits where they are safely able to function in a process known as geofencing (Synopsys, 2020). In 2018, an AV company by the name of Waymo announced a driverless taxi service in Phoenix, Arizona that would operate in four suburbs: Chandler, Tempe, Gilbert and Mesa (Sage, 2018). Initially, this service had a human behind the wheel to ensure a safe trip for passengers but, by the end of 2020 it was able to transition to a near driverless experience with each vehicle having a remote supervisor to assist when more dynamic situations would arise. For example, a Waymo spokesperson explained to Lee (2020) ‘if a Waymo vehicle detects that a road ahead may be closed due to construction, it can pull over and request a second set of eyes’. Waymo’s choice to start rolling out their service in these Phoenix suburbs is not an irrelevant detail as weather conditions play a big part in how well AVs operate.

In the next 3 years and further moving forward, large amounts of autonomous vehicles are expected to be rapidly deployed around the world with the rise of sensor technologies and Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs). ABI Research (2018) predicts that approximately 8 million vehicles featuring level 3, 4 and 5 autonomous functions will be shipped in 2025 and available to the mass market. While the vehicles featuring level 3 and 4 AV functions may still need drivers due to certain driving conditions requiring human intervention, vehicles with level 5 autonomy will be able to operate completely autonomously with no driver intervention at all.

Other sources, such as Underwood (2014), predict that AVs in 2025 may be able to operate on all forms of road – whether it be highway, urban or rural surfaces. They also predict full level 5 autonomy on all surfaces by at least 2035, effectively eliminating humans from behind the wheel. Wardrop (2009) proposes that by 2022, 30% of all trucks operating in the United Kingdom have the potential to be fully automated while IEEE (2012) estimates that up to 75% of all vehicles on the road could be fully autonomous by 2040. In the transport and haulage industry, trucks and buses may be the first to see autonomous replacements as the routes taken are far less dynamic than those taken by private commuters.

More and more countries are beginning to legalise the use of AVs on their roads such as the UK, Sweden, and the US. In 2017, Sweden launched the Drive Me project which saw 100 Volvo AVs go to families in Gothenburg. These vehicles were for use on public roads and did not require continuous supervision, allowing the driver to perform other tasks during their trips (Victor et al., 2017). With the research gathered from these countries, there may be more AVs on roads for our use sooner rather than later.

The quick rise and development of AVs for use on roads is possible due to advancements in technologies such as LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) sensors. LiDAR has the potential to provide reliable obstacle detection, coupled with the deployment of various Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) packages by OEMs.

The technological developments of vehicle automation potentially can transform the workforce and impact jobs. The potential impact of automation on employment varies by occupation and sector, with more physical jobs in predictable environments being the most susceptible. These may include operating machinery and preparing fast food. Two more industries that may be affected by automation are collecting and processing data, with jobs such as mortgage origination, paralegal work, accounting, and back-office transaction processing seeing a significant decrease in available work (McKinsey & Co, 2017) due to the evolution of not just vehicle automation, but automation as a whole.

AVs could cause a rise in energy consumption levels when they become the prevalent mode of transport. This predicted rise may be caused by an increased demand for travel due to the on-demand mobility they offer, which in turn increases the amount of energy used by AVs. Another driving factor for this rise may be because of the accessibility these vehicles provide, allowing people who do not drive as much or not have been driving for a period of time to make use of the services offered by AVs.

At the same time, AVs may be able to help energy consumption due to efficient driving methods, through a concept known as platooning. Platooning is when multiple vehicles all operate in a tight group together which helps to reduce forces imposed upon the vehicles (especially those in the center) while driving. This method of driving is not possible for humans due to the reaction times required to brake without causing a crash. AVs would not have this issue due to their ability to communicate and execute commands based on their surroundings (Wadud, MacKenzie & Leiby, 2016).

The establishment of AVs as the conventional method of both private and public transportation is still several years from fruition. Though level 5 autonomy is on the horizon, current AVs are only able to operate in a limited capacity – whether this limit be geofenced areas or the requirement of constant human oversight. As the adoption rate of AVs increases, positives and negatives may become more apparent in day-to-day life. Some such benefits may include a decrease in traffic congestion, increased mobility for people with disabilities or the elderly, and an overall safer experience for both pedestrians and drivers. With over 90% of crashes caused by human error (NHTSA, 2015) the adoption of AVs has the potential for a large positive impact on road safety due to the communicative ability they possess with each other. However, accidents are not always avoidable.

AVs may encounter ethical issues relating to the safety of the driver versus the safety of pedestrians in impact scenarios. In these types of scenarios, a computer may make unpredictable decisions and end up causing more harm than a human driver. While there are benefits to consider, there are also negative aspects of AVs, such as the threat of cybersecurity related attacks. A person with malicious intent may be able to compromise an AV to disable safety features and cause an impact, steal private information such as where an individual may live or work, or reroute the destination of the AV. Due to the relatively small demand for AVs, another factor that is against them is the cost. Without a large network of AVs in operation and because they rely on communication within a network, congestion mitigation could be completely nullified due to the lack of information available. Weather and terrain may also have an adverse effect on AVs, with the functionality being reduced or even fully negated due to rain, snow or uneven terrain (Anderson et al., 2014). Until these issues are investigated further, autonomous driving may still be years away from being available to the public at large.


What we see today as “The Cloud” and specifically cloud computing is the progression of a technical, economic and social evolution.’ (Oppitz, Marcus, and Peter Tomsu 2017, p 2) It really started with the invention of the World Wide Web otherwise known by most people these days as “The Internet”. The Internet has made it possible to provide on-demand technology services to a worldwide audience. As technology progressed over the years giving us faster computers and faster internet access, these two advances have given us access to Cloud Computing. The Cloud delivers an integrated suite of functions and services to its users through workflows and virtualisation delivered over the Internet. It uses IT Infrastructure as an on-demand service and can be defined in three distinct layers. The Software as a Service (SaaS) layer where you do not manage anything and just access the Application front-end, examples of which include online email clients such as Gmail and Office tools such as Office 365. Platform as a Service (PaaS) which is used for developing and deploying software and services where you manage the Application and the Data, examples of which are Windows Azure, Heroku and Google App Engine. Thirdly the Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) where you are managing everything down to the OS level and Runtime environment, examples of which are Rackspace, Amazon AWS and Microsoft Azure. With such large organisations now providing Cloud services and competing with each other for private and government contracts alike, more capital is being invested in the development of Cloud services which will benefit all consumers. In November 2018, The Wall Street Journal stated that “there’s no end in sight to the cloud’s growth: By 2024, the global cloud computing market is expected to surpass $1 trillion.” (Foutty and Bawa 2018, para 5).

From a technical viewpoint, Cloud systems comprise of processing, network and storage elements. There are 3 primary deployment models for Cloud Computing. The first is the Public Cloud. This is the cloud we are most familiar with as it hosts the majority of currently used cloud services including Dropbox and Facebook. A connection to the Public cloud is accessing data that is hosted in a data centre which is managed by a third-party provider over the internet. In a public cloud system, there are many organisations sharing the same IT infrastructure. The second is the Private Cloud which is primarily used by companies that are concerned with storing their data on publicly accessible servers. This system uses servers and hardware owned by the company and access can be provided either over the internet or over a private internal network. The last model is a Hybrid Cloud model which uses a combination of models 1 and 2. An example of which would be a company that uses a Hybrid email system with some accounts hosted on Office 365 and others hosted internally on their own Exchange servers. Communication is established between the two systems and they function as one hybrid email system.

As Cloud computing is on demand and is provided from servers globally, it can be flexible in the delivery of storage and computing power growing and contracting with your business needs. This is referred to as elasticity in business terms as the service can literally contract and expand with your needs. As your business needs change, you can access cloud services to store or back up data, process data or to stream videos for training or meeting purposes, on a pay as you use pricing structure. This also can eliminate or reduce the capital set up costs of buying hardware and software, as well as the need for onsite infrastructure, further reducing business costs such as in physical space, electricity, and staffing. In most cases, you only pay for what you use, further lowering business costs and allowing for more flexibility.

Cloud computing has had a dramatic impact on society today. It has also had a largely positive impact on businesses and organisations by increasing their revenue and helping them achieve their goals. It provides technology at reduced cost, by using resources on demand only with zero upfront costs. Cloud technology is also highly scalable according to the needs of an organisation. Companies no longer need to worry about future growth as the technology can easily adapt and scale with their growth. As for the everyday person, cloud technology has provided us with IOT devices connecting our homes in smart ways, cloud storage for our backups and our photos, social media to connect with family and friends over great distances as well as providing us with Online Learning capabilities which have now become mainstream in Universities all over the world. Especially in these days with threats like COVID-19, cloud computing has had a major impact on the Education word. It provided remote learning and video conferencing tools to staff and students during a lockdown allowing the continuation of studies without the need to attend school.


Cybersecurity has fast become one of the most important facets of Information Technology and this is largely to do with the fact that there has been a significant growth in the industry over the last decade. As technology advances, so does societal dependency. It is becoming increasingly difficult to avoid such advancements, which is why IT professionals from all around the world have recognised the impact of cybercrime and are implementing protective measures for individuals and organisations against malicious activities.

Cybersecurity incidents occur frequently and are not discriminative, in the fact that they target anyone. Currently, there are numerous methods of counteracting malicious attacks, but they boil down to two main categories, those being reactive and proactive countermeasures.

Reactive cybersecurity methods are those that have been implemented to detect a threat already inside your host system or network, such as a Breach Detection Systems (BDS). Whereas a proactive cybersecurity measures are used to pre-empt an attack on a computer system or network and one example of this is Virtual Dispersive Networking (VDN).

VDN is a preventative approach to cybercrime as it enables data segmentation during transmission. This means that the data packet has been broken into several segments that use separate optimal paths for transmission. In the event of an eavesdropping or man-in-the-middle (MIM) attack, only a portion of the data is at risk of interception.

Cyber security in its infancy was response driven, largely waiting for a cyber-attack to occur before implementing counteractive measures and new computing standards. In order to reduce the ever-present risk of cybercrime, it is important to introduce proactive measures that analyse the network and predict malware.

The majority of security professionals these days use Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) to monitor the constant flow of attacks and respond as needed. However, this is unsustainable due to the demand it places on the workforce. It is also difficult to gain expertise in this way as the turnover for such jobs is so large. A much better way to combat this would be to use behaviour analysis combined with AI and deep learning to automate some of the work and highlight alerts for the attention of the security monitors. An example of this is Veksler and Buchler’s (2016) proposal for a Symbolic Deep Learning System which can be used to construct alternative user models. Their method is able to classify a large set of small data into models that can be used for security analysis.

With the rise of multi-factor authentication systems and embedded hardware authentication, companies such as Google, Microsoft, Lastpass and Yubikey are leading the charge in making security more user-friendly and mainstream. Their efforts help to increase our defences against hackers trying to access our emails, personal data and bank accounts.

Technological developments are one way that we will be better positioned to combat Cyber-threats. Identity management with the use of Apples Touch-ID and Face-ID technologies are just one example of this currently in use today. It is possible that 20 years from now, we will not need to rely on these devices to store our identity but rather through the use of Biometric implants. Of course, technologies such as that carry with it an inherent distrust and risk of abuse by governments and organisations. That said, the increased rampant threat of identity threat and fraud may be enough to sway public opinion.

The movement towards IPv6 brings with it not just a substantial increase in the number of IP addresses available, but also several security enhancements as well. These include mandatory IPSEC, mandatory multicast, mandatory QoS, no more NAT and many new rules for network technologies. However, the adoption of IPv6 faces many hurdles and since it’s largely new code it’s likely to have many flaws and vulnerabilities in the early stages. Regardless, ISPs are already deploying IPv6 capable routers and services so adoption of this new technology may happen sooner than later.

Machine AI and Deep Learning may be one of the key technologies in the future that can be better suited to detect and stop cyber threats. They are able to collate and compare many sources of data and be far more accurate than human eyes in identifying the characteristics and signatures of threats allowing us to better respond to their persistent attacks.

“Our society, economy, and critical infrastructures have become largely dependent on computer networks and information technology solutions” (Jang-Jaccard & Nepal 2014). This reliance has created both risks and opportunities and underscores the value of cyber security in professional and personal contexts. When an organisation is reactive to threats or breaches rather than proactive, the likely impacts include data loss, exposure of private or sensitive information, reputational damage, potential fines due to the breaches as well as damages and costs to clients and suppliers as a consequence of these breaches.

Inadequate security by an organisation that stores personal or sensitive information that is then attacked, can have wide reaching and long-lasting consequences.

In July 2015, the theft of personal information from a dating website for people who are married or in relationships, Ashley Madison (or ALM) made global headlines when users' data was leaked online on searchable platforms. The reported fallout included divorces and suicides as personal data, including names, account transactions, addresses and personal messages were made public. Before leaking the data, the group responsible, known as the Impact Team, did not ask for money, but demanded that the parent company, Avid Dating Life close the site. When they “dumped” the data, they wrote: “It was ALM that failed you and lied to you. Prosecute them and claim damages. Then move on with your life.” (Zetter 2015 para 18). Some of those affected did just that. In July 2017, ALM agreed to settle two dozen lawsuits for $11.2 million.

This example shows that users may believe that they are protected, but if the organisation has inadequate protections in place, all are vulnerable.

With advances in Cyber Security some older technologies that have known vulnerabilities and issues, for example - web servers and programming languages and libraries, will become redundant. New jobs will be created in the development of new technologies and cyber security has become a growth area within the Information Technology field.

The Ernest and Young Global Information Security Survey of 2018 –19 estimated a global shortfall of about 1.8 million security professionals within five years. It also identified that to adequately fill these positions, women and minorities need to be a recruitment focus as “Diverse teams drive better results across the organization. They are more innovative, objective and collaborative. That’s critical in cybersecurity where every day is a fight to stay a step ahead of the attackers” (van Kessel 2018, p.7).

Implementing state of the art technology to help impede cybersecurity incidents is essential to maintain positive control over our personal data. However, without the education to support such technologies, it would be unreasonable to suggest there will be a decline in cybercrime activities in the future.

Educating your friends and family on the tell-tale signs of suspicious activity is the single most important response to mitigating the vast effects of a cybersecurity incident such as Phishing. Phishing is a phrase used to describe an email that is disguised to hide malware. Such emails are designed to look appealing to the recipient, in order to trick them into providing personal financial details or to click on a link that contains a scraping tool. According to an examination conducted by Verizon, 94% of malware was delivered through email in 2019 (Verizon, 2019). A statistic this high suggests that this method of delivery is effective in its intent to cause disruption or damage to networks and individuals and for its ease of distribution allowing significant global coverage.

Intellectual property and other personal details such as financial or medical records are worth keeping protected. The most common type of protection available is in the form of a password. But far too often, bad password habits and a lack of password knowledge can cause significant vulnerabilities in your system, putting all your hard work and personal details at risk. Password Complexity Recommendations is a report that outlines how new and upcoming technology can be used to crack passwords using hash. The report suggests that using an 11-character password, containing a combination of uppercase, lowercase, symbols, and numbers will add complexity to any good password (Defence Research and Development Canada, 2014).

Technology should not solely be relied upon to create a safe haven for personal data or our most intellectual thoughts. Instead, educating family and friends about safe online practices may one day lead to lower cybercrime statistics.


A Raspberry Pi (RPi) is a Single Board Computer (SBC) and is typically the size of a credit card, easily being able to fit into your pocket. However, this is not recommended without first fitting it with a case to protect the internal electrical components. The RPi was first developed in the University of Cambridge’s Computer Laboratory in 2006 as an affordable computing platform for children to learn programming on (Ortmeyer, 2014). There are a number of different models within the RPi family, with the latest model – the Raspberry Pi 4 – having both USB 2.0 and 3.0 ports, an ethernet port, inbuilt wireless adapter, USB-C power supply input, a 40-pin general-purpose input/ouput (GPIO) pinout and micro-SD slot for storage purposes. The latest model, the Raspberry Pi 400, even comes with a mini keyboard built into the unit. Most commonly, RPi’s are bought alongside starter kits which contain all the components required to get it up and running. There are also more advanced kits that add camera modules which have the possibility for real world implementation.

One example of this is a parking space management system proposed by Vítek and Melničuk (2017). In this scenario, a network of wireless cameras with RPi Zeros connected to each camera would relay positional data of vehicles back to a cloud server which then relays the potential vacancy of a parking spot to a client application. The RPi can also be used as a replacement for your desktop or laptop computer though there may be a significant performance decrease in favour of a smaller form factor. The RPi is often used in robotics, allowing users to program them for autonomous interaction with other technology.

Though it was initially developed to encourage students to learn more about computer science, the RPi was able to sell 10 million units by 2016 with the CEO of the Raspberry Pi Foundation stating, ‘we’ve beaten our wildest dreams by three orders of magnitude, and we’re only just getting started’ (Upton, 2016). Though there have been SBCs before the RPi, they were often used in industrial settings in things like vending machines (Johnston and Cox, 2017). Due to the low price and availability of the RPi, there have been a myriad of experiments and prototypes created using its technology.

The RPi is a popular SBC among the hobbyist crowd due to the ease in which it can be integrated with current devices to convert them to smart objects. Some examples of these projects include linking an RPI to a smart phone app to open a garage door (Coward, 2020), a home media centre (Parecki, 2020) and an ad blocker for your home network (Pi-hole, 2021). Besides in-home use, the RPi has also seen use in the construction of interactive art. In 2013, the University of Southampton featured an exhibit titled Erica the Rhino as part of a campaign to raise awareness for rhino conservation. In this exhibit, the audience was able to interact with a sculpture of a rhino by stroking it, feeding it or even sending it tweets (Basford et al., 2016). Through this interaction, Erica was able to produce an output based on what element the audience interacted with. This interactivity was achieved with a few RPi units, some sensors and some programming. Another facet of RPi use is for educational purposes, with several courses on offer through the Raspberry Pi website for students to learn programming.

As the Raspberry Pi (RPi) is an inexpensive piece of hardware it has helped revolutionise the education system. The devices basic computing functionality paired with its potential for further programming, will allow schools and universities alike, to manipulate the current curriculum to accommodate numerous learning styles. One such university has already implemented the use of a RPi in their Undergraduate Physics degree. The RPi has been programmed with an open-source software package known as the PhyPiDAQ, which essentially allows the students manage data acquisition using sensory control over digital inputs and outputs. This software also permits students to configure the RPi is ways necessary for them to complete a specific component of an experiment, such as, sensor position, acceleration, temperature, current and force while having the ability to display these findings through GUI in real time (Wong, Quast & Braig, 2020). The fact that RPi’s can be used as an interactive approach to learning with the added ability of repetitive use shows the usefulness of such devices.

Another study has shown, that RPi have been tested in conjunction with the cloud to assist law enforcement officers in identifying suspects using facial recognition (Sajjad et al. 2020). The trail successfully utilised the RPi 3 model B, using 2.7 Python as the programming language. As RPi has limited resources such as storage, processing power and memory, so it was paired with a cloud service to counteract the technological shortfall. Law Enforcement officers used a small wireless camera that processes live camera footage through the RPi for facial recognition. The program then uses other technologies such as a support vector machine, which helped identify suspects (Sajjad et al. 2020).

This is just two examples on how the RPi can effect change on a large scale if used appropriately. As RPi’s are still relativity juvenile, it will be interesting to see what it can do in the future, to really unlock the potential of human capacity.

For your average gaming enthusiast, the Raspberry Pi has given new meaning to old school gaming by officially reviving ‘retro’ gaming consoles. Currently, the RPi can be used to power the console that hosts fan-favourite past-time games, all emulated within the retro-pie interface. It encourages intergeneration participation, one great example, is uniting my family together, especially my father and grandfather as we explore our favourite past times together.

Not only does RPi rebirth retro gaming, but it can also be used as a very low-cost computer. Nowadays, purchasing a basic computer or laptop for a school age student can be expensive, especially when you need to take other expenses into consideration, like, school uniforms and all those other back-to-school basics. A RPi can generate enough power for basic computing requirements, such as basic applications to conduct homework or school assignments. Leisurely, it also acts as the perfect platform for you to teach your niece or nephew about programming languages or introduce them to Minecraft and Minecraft Mods.

It is interesting to see how the RPi has developed and what technologies it has influenced over the last decade but more so in its contribution to the global COVID-19 pandemic. The pandemic itself has seen the IT industry expand to meet rising demands, but more so in the medical department of Columbian hospitals, where the RPi was used to help control ventilators of COVID patients (Thomas, 2020). The RPi was designed to control the air pressure, open and close valves and regulate breathing assistance. If this small single board computer can make changes that will aid patients in a pandemic, it is safe to say that this technology will contribute to further developments in the future. Who knows, it might just save your life or a life of a loved one.

Project Idea


Caffeina

For the love of coffee


Overview

Caffeina is a user-friendly application to monitor water intake based on caffeine consumed for the fitness and health-conscious individual. Conway (2020) states that coffee is one of the most popular drinks consumed worldwide, the goal is to ensure that users are keeping up with recommended water intake to compensate for the diuretic effect that caffeine can cause. This ensures optimum health and hopefully relieves any guilt of caffeine consumption for the health conscious. Caffeina will be designed to be user friendly and simplistic, not wanting to add to your health routine, but enhance it. It will provide the user with notifications and rewards to help keep them on track and motivated to continue using the application.

Motivation

Caffeina was inspired by the developer’s love affair with coffee and her interest in wearable technology and fitness. The goal was to create a user friendly application to seamlessly integrate into the user's daily fitness and health routine. She was motivated by the idea that technology can enhance the users' journey to a healthy lifestyle as well as enhance their overall wellbeing. She, along with the development team, wanted to ensure that the application use did not feel like a chore for the user, but was a fun enhancement to their lifestyle.

Description

The Application

Caffeina will be available on either iOS or Android and can be downloaded for free from your respective app store. Using a smart device, connected to the internet, simply download the application and create an account. To simplify the account creation process, it can be done one of two ways, either linking an existing account from Facebook, Google and Apple or by manually entering the required data into the respective fields. If the user decides to keep the account separate from pre-existing accounts, a valid email address and a strong password will be required. On successful login, three additional questions will present to the user, such as, gender, height and weight. This will help personalise the account and increase the accuracy of the data that is sent to the user - in the form of a caffeine consumption calculator.

Once logged in, the first page that will appear to the user will be the coffee selection page. This page will contain three interactive lists that are titled, Caffè, Milk and Sugar. The Caffè list will contain common types of coffees such as Espresso, Ristretto, Instant, Caffè Latte, Flat White, Cappuccino, Mocha and Americano. As the user selects the desired type of coffee, the ‘Milk’ list will become active. This only occurs if the user selects a coffee that contains a milk-based product. The user can then select what type of milk they want, low-fat, full cream, almond, coconut and so on. As the ‘Milk’ is selected the application will then ask the user if they would like sugar, in a simple yes or no icon. Lastly the user will be able to select the size of the drink they are consuming, such as Espresso, Cappuccino, Latte, even Venti and Grande.

On completion of the selected item, the application will generate a coffee icon in the middle of the page, which has been programmed to allow easy user interaction. For instance, double tapping the icon, indicates that the user has ordered a double shot coffee. Don’t feel like caffeine today, swipe the icon to the left to specify Decaf. Decided to have a late afternoon coffee but don’t want to be up all night, hold the icon down until it flashes and then slide down, selecting half strength.

Another amazing feature is the automatic caffeine to rehydration tally. This indicates how much water the user needs to consume to level out the diuretic effect that coffee can have on the human body. Located at the top of the page, are small coffee cups that indicate how many coffees you have consumed that day. By simply tapping on the icon, it shows the user how much water they need to consume. Once the water has been consumed, the user can tick off the cup and it will fade indicating that this challenge has been completed. Don’t worry, if you forget to drink the required amount of water in the specified time period, a push notification will be sent to your phone to remind you.

For the application to work flawlessly, the caffeine to water consumption needs to be regulated by a formula. For every 100mg of caffeine consumed an additional cup of water (250ml) should be consumed to compensate for the diuretic effect (SFGATE 2018). The following example will provide an insight on how this is calculated on the server side of the application.

1 x Espresso (shot only - 30ml) = 63.6mg of caffeine

63.6 ÷ 100 = 0.636

0.636 * 250mL = 159mL

Based on this example, the consumer will need to consume 0.7 cups of water to remain hydrated after consuming an Espresso. In order to display this information to the user in an easy to read and interpret format, cups will be either display in a half or full capacity, equating to, 1 x Espresso = 1 cup of water.

The application will also be able to provide a statistical summary for the user of their coffee consumption filtered daily, weekly, or monthly. Using this data, the application can provide feedback to the user warning them if they have consumed too much coffee over a certain period.

Basic Application Designs

Coffee Version Release:

Each eversion release will contain a scheduled update that will improve overall useability, bug fixing and software assessments to ensure the integrity and validity of the product has been maintained. Time and dates for each release, will be available online, prior to the update going live.

Version II

Tech Inception will expand the Caffè selection range and introduce an improved list of milk types. Also, in version 2, you can expect to see a new tab that introduces the Favourites Menu. This menu is automatically generated by using analytical data gathered from its userbase. This will help streamline the process and get you rehydrated faster.

Version III

Caffeina lovers can expect to see an added calorie counting feature in the V3 release. Tech Inception, paired with the expertise of their inhouse Nutritionist, Daniela, will research the number of calories included in each item on the selected menu. To make this user friendly, a formula will be used to convert kilojoules to calories to give the consumer real time information upfront, in an easy to interpret format.

Version IV

Coffee selection and calorie counting just got easier with barcode scanning. Using your current smart device, simply scan the product barcode to receive in app, real time feedback. This feature also supports the recommended water consumption required to maintain hydration l House on registration. After the user selects the coffee and the size, they specify how much sugar or extra milk they want and mark the coffee as Consumed.

Version V

Tech Inceptions biggest release yet. Introducing Caffeina to wearables. The application will now be supported on the Apple Watch and Android Smartwatches. This cross-platform model is designed to allow basic application interactions only, for instances, ticking off your coffee cups to indicate you have completed that rehydration challenge and to check the progress of the user's other goals.

Version VI

Tech Inception has started making headwind for competitors with the Version VI release of Siri and Google Assistant integration. The user can now use voice commands to interact with the application. For example, user - “Hey Siri, I just drank a cup of coffee”. Siri - “Your required water consumption is 250ml”. This enables a handsfree approach to the application, allowing the user to continue with their busy schedule.

Version VII

Version VII will introduce an NFC support module, allowing users to bump their watch or phone against an imbedded NFC tag to increase the coffee count instead of tapping the icon on their device. Tech Inception could start selling accessories with NFC tags pre-inserted, these items could include coffee cups/travel mugs, coasters or even stickers. Once the user has finished their cup of coffee, they would contact one of these NFC compatible items using either their Apple Watch or smartphone to record the drink. These tags would have to be pre-programmed before shipping, thus limiting the kind of coffee it could record to only one type per tag.

Other Proposed Future Developments

In order to make the most out of the application and support local businesses, Tech Inception have decided to link our application to various coffee stores. This can be done in a variety of ways. One, members from Tech Inception visit local coffee stores and ask them to sign up for registration on our application or two, registration for more remote stores can be done online. Registering with our applications only requires a few details, including company name, coffee types and coffee ratios unique to their store. This data will be entered into the Caffeina database which is cloud stored.

How the store benefits – when a Caffeina user enters your store using our application, NFC, QR Codes or location data will present the user with unique coffee selections relevant to that store. An example scenario would be as follows: Caffeina user walks into Coffee House (local Coffee Business). The app uses their location to determine where they are and displays the name ‘Coffee House’ at the top. It will also list all the coffees currently available in store. The user then decides to order a Cappuccino and the application then shows the available sizes being a Regular or Large. When the user selects regular, the application already knows the size of the cup including the coffee to water ratio content according to the data provided by our database.

Obstacles and Drawbacks

Platform Development

Picking the right platform for the initial launch of Caffeina may require some thought. In Australia, the most popular mobile OS is Apple’s iOS claiming a 54% share compared to Android’s 45%. When compared to the worldwide stats however, this changes to Android at 72% and iOS at 26% (StatCounter Global Stats, 2020). Do we develop for the broader platform in Australia, or cater to the larger worldwide demographic?

Developing for a specific platform has its pros and cons, but so does developing for cross-platform use. Cross-platform apps are generally easier to maintain but may not offer much integration with other apps and services. Platform specific apps allow integration with other services like Siri or Google Assistant, as well as increased optimisation. Although, it would also require several different versions of the app due to the varied nature of the Android platform. Early prototypes may be developed with the cross-platform method in mind, allowing the features to be added and iterated upon until they are polished enough for Version V, the wearables update. This version requires specific platform development to function as detailed.

Currently, no members of Tech Inception have experience with developing mobile apps. This may require a prototype version of Caffeina to be coded and deployed as a very basic program or webpage, or for Tech Inception to seek outside assistance with later versions of Caffeina.

Performance vs. Battery Life

Caffeina will not be a resource demanding app due to its relatively simplistic nature, but some considerations will still need to be made with regards to performance and battery life. For example, the app does not need to be constantly running in the background to store the cups of coffee you have had throughout the day and only needs to retrieve and modify this data when another cup of coffee is consumed. Such data would be stored locally instead of in the cloud to prevent unnecessary battery usage. This would also increase the responsiveness of the app as it would not need to download the data to modify it.

Accuracy of Data

While the data used by Tech Inception will consist of accurate caffeine measurements based on regular cup sizes and formulas based on these sizes, the user may not enter their data correctly. If the user is having coffee at home, they could unintentionally brew a weaker/stronger coffee or have a larger than usual mug, thus causing the recommended amount of water suggested to be inaccurate.

Hardware

Caffeina is a free iOS or Android app and requires an up-to-date device to make use of all its features. For optimal functionality of the application, it is recommended that users, use a phone that was developed within the last 5 years, and contains the most recent update.

Software / Tools

React Native

While there are obstacles that may stand in the way of Tech Inception developing Caffeina, there are also solutions to these problems. React Native is a cross platform development kit that uses JavaScript. It is used by many popular apps such as Facebook, Instagram, Discord, Pinterest and many more. One of its main strengths is the ability to write modules in C, Swift and Java, as well as work on image and video processing directly. It is one of the best platforms to use as it is very fast for converting your code into native elements quickly. Among some other big benefits for using React Native are Lower Development costs because we would be developing for both iOS and Android at the same time requiring less developers. There is also a large developer community for React and since it primarily uses JavaScript it will be much easier to hire a developer since Swift is much less popular. Lastly React can also be used to create a web application version for the app. Not something needed initially but worth considering down the line. React does not support the creation of wearable device apps from either iOS or Android so other development tools must be used for that such as XCode.
